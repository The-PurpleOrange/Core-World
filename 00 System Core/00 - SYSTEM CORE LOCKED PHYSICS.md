# Core World Rules

---

# World Core Concept

A world comprised of a core, orbited by islands in a sea of cloud.

The planet’s exposed core is the primary source of **light and heat**.

Civilizations live on floating islands suspended above the core.  
The habitable region exists above a vast **global cloud sea** that behaves similarly to an ocean surface.

Most human life exists and travels between these islands in the several atmospheric bands above the cloud layer where life thrives.

---

# Surface Illumination

Illumination of the surface is primarily produced by the diffused light and heat from the core shining through the cloud sea, as well as reflections and scattering from the upper cloud layer.

It has several modifiers, as things such as denser phases in the secondary shadowbelt for seasonal changes, as well as cloud sea density.

Due to this the light level on the surface alternates daily between; a full day bright soft and diffuse shadows during the day, to a permanent nautical twilight at night.

---

# Seasonal Light System

Seasonal changes occur because of **two particulate belts orbiting between the core and the cloud sea layer**.

## Primary Shadowbelt
A dense orbital particulate ring regularly blocks the core.
This produces the **day and night cycle** experienced across the entire world.

## Uneven Outer Shadowbelt
A second belt with irregular density slowly rotates relative to the core.
As dense or thin regions of this belt pass between the islands and the core, the amount of light reaching the world changes.

This produces the **planet’s seasonal light cycle** and drives the following natural processes.
- convection strength
- storm intensity and seasons
- vegetation growth periods
- seasonal travel conditions

---

## Calendar Structure

### Day
Defined by the passage of the **primary shadowbelt**.

### Turning
One full seasonal cycle.
Length is approximately **280 days**

### Cycle
Seven Turnings form a **Cycle**.
A Cycle represents the time required for major drifting islands on the Tradewinds Belt to complete a global circuit.

# Floating Islands

Floating islands remain suspended because their rock contains **lift mineral** that converts core radiation into a gravity-opposing force.

This mineral forms a microscopic filament network throughout rock and soil and allows islands to maintain stable altitude bands above the cloud sea.

Large crystal deposits act as energy collectors and store core radiation energy.

# Water Cycle

The cloud sea acts as the planet’s **primary water reservoir**.

Water reaches the islands through several mechanisms:

- fog lift rising from the cloud layer
- storm updraft spray
- condensation and rainfall from cloud towers

Water retention on islands depends heavily on soil depth, vegetation, and wind exposure.

# Island Types

Floating islands behave differently depending on how they interact with atmospheric circulation.

## Anchored Islands

Large stable landmasses sitting in calm atmospheric zones.

Characteristics

- minimal drift
- stable climates
- oldest civilizations

---

## Cluster Islands

Groups of islands drifting slowly together.

Characteristics

- shared ecosystems
- gradual migration across the world
- function like floating archipelagos

---

## Belt Islands

Islands captured by major wind lanes.

Characteristics

- predictable migration routes
- primary trade corridors
- frequent encounters with other islands

---

## Drifter Islands

Small fragments moving between circulation systems.

Characteristics

- irregular movement
- unpredictable encounters
- isolated ecosystems

---

# Island Spacing

Island density varies widely.

Large islands are typically separated by **hundreds of kilometers**.

Small islands (1–2 km²) may cluster much closer together, sometimes **5–50 km apart**, forming dense swarms.

Direct contact between islands is rare, but **close passes occur frequently**, especially within drifting belts.

---

# Ecosystem Survival

Island ecosystems do not require direct contact to exchange life.

Species spread through:

- wind-borne seeds and spores
- birds and flying animals
- storm transport
- drifting debris

Large island belts tend to share common ecosystems, while isolated islands evolve unique species.

---

# Atmospheric Wind Belts

The atmosphere organizes into several large **wind circulation belts** driven by:

- constant heating from the planetary core
- convection from the cloud sea
- planetary rotation

These belts act like **ocean currents in the sky**, gradually moving floating islands across the world.

They determine:

- island drift directions
- trade routes
- storm formation regions
- long-term migration of island belts

The full structure of the atmospheric circulation system is described in:

- Global Wind Belts

---

# Navigational Reality

Because islands migrate through atmospheric currents, navigation and trade depend heavily on predicting where islands will drift over time.

Sky navigators track wind belts, seasonal shifts, and island migration patterns to determine future positions.

Travelers often care less about where an island **is**, and more about **where it will be** when they arrive.

---

# World Structure Analogy

The sky behaves like an ocean.

- wind belts act as currents
- island clusters resemble archipelagos
- drifting fragments behave like floating debris
- stable islands resemble seamounts

Maps must track **time as well as position**, because islands move slowly through the atmosphere.

---

# Flavor Elements

The undersides of islands are dangerous regions used for mining.

Explorers rely on specialized equipment such as:

- grapple launchers
- jump harnesses
- gliders
- landing gear

Common sky-sailor curse phrase:

**“Currents and swells.”**

# Current World Identity

A drifting sky-ocean planet where:

- floating islands migrate through atmospheric currents
- civilizations adapt to moving geography
- climate is governed by corelight and cloud oceans
- the sky itself behaves like a living ocean
