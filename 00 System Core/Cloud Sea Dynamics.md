## Overview

The Cloud Sea is a dense global cloud layer that functions as the planet’s primary water reservoir and atmospheric boundary.

It separates the habitable floating island layer from the hotter regions closer to the planetary core.

From above, the cloud sea behaves similarly to an ocean surface, with waves, currents, storms, and rising moisture.

---

## Vertical Structure

The cloud sea forms a thick atmospheric layer with three functional zones.

Upper Cloud Boundary  
The top of the cloud layer where floating islands typically stabilize.

Properties
- bright scattered core light
- heavy fog and mist
- strong vertical convection
- frequent condensation and rainfall

This is where most habitable islands sit.

---

Cloud Interior  
Dense storm cloud region.

Properties
- extremely poor visibility
- heavy turbulence
- strong vertical updrafts
- electrical storms common

Travel through this layer is dangerous and rare.

---

Lower Cloud Boundary  
The lower edge of the cloud sea, closer to the core.

Properties
- hotter air
- rising vapor columns
- strong convection currents

These rising currents feed moisture upward into the cloud sea.

---

## Water Cycle

Water constantly circulates between the cloud sea and the islands above it.

Primary mechanisms:

Fog Lift  
Fine mist slowly rises above the cloud layer and settles on island surfaces.

Storm Updraft Spray  
Powerful storms throw droplets and vapor far above the cloud tops.

Cloudfall Rain  
Condensed water occasionally falls from the upper cloud layer onto islands.

Because islands are exposed to wind and sunlight, water retention depends heavily on soil depth and vegetation.

---

## Atmospheric Currents

Large scale convection from the heated lower atmosphere produces rising air columns.

This motion feeds:

- high altitude wind belts
- island drift currents
- storm formation zones

Where multiple convection systems meet, large storm corridors form.

---

## Cloud Surface Behavior

The top of the cloud sea behaves somewhat like a fluid surface.

Features include:

- rolling cloud waves
- storm towers rising above the cloud surface
- calm basins with thin cloud cover
- violent updraft regions

These patterns influence where islands can safely exist.

---

## Island Interaction

Floating islands interact constantly with the cloud layer.

Common effects include:

Cloud Edge Fog  
Islands sitting near the cloud surface experience frequent fog and mist.

Moisture Capture  
Vegetation and rock surfaces collect moisture directly from passing clouds.

Storm Impact  
Storm towers rising from the cloud sea can strike islands directly.

These interactions create highly variable local climates.

---

## Sub-Cloud Region

Below the cloud sea lies a hotter and more hostile atmospheric region.

Characteristics

- strong updraft columns
- poor visibility
- intense heat and turbulence
- drifting debris and metal-rich bodies

Ore-rich meteors and debris accumulate here because they lack lift mineral and cannot remain suspended above the cloud layer.

Mining operations occasionally descend into this region to recover materials.

---

## Climatic Role

The cloud sea regulates planetary climate by:

- storing the majority of atmospheric water
- distributing heat through convection
- driving wind systems that move islands

Without the cloud sea, the floating island layer would experience far more extreme temperature swings and much lower humidity.

---

## Summary

The cloud sea acts as the planet’s atmospheric ocean.

It provides:

- the main water source
- storm generation
- atmospheric circulation
- a boundary layer supporting floating islands

Most life in the world exists in the narrow region just above this cloud layer where light, moisture, and stable temperatures overlap.
