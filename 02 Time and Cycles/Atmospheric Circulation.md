## Overview  
  
The planet’s atmosphere is driven primarily by **temperature differences between the bright pole and the dark pole**.  
  
The permanent bright pole receives constant radiant heating from the core, while the permanent dark pole remains in shadow and acts as a cold sink.  
  
This temperature imbalance creates large-scale atmospheric circulation that moves heat and moisture across the planet.  
  
However, circulation is not a single continuous flow.    
Instead, the atmosphere forms **stable wind belts and circulating cells** that move air in multiple directions.  
  
These wind belts are a defining feature of the planet’s environment and strongly influence island drift, weather patterns, and climate zones.  
  
---  
  
## Primary Atmospheric Engine  
  
The main circulation driver is the **hot-to-cold pressure gradient**.  
  
Process:  
  
1. Air near the **bright pole heats and rises**  
2. Rising air spreads outward in the upper atmosphere  
3. As the air cools, it descends toward colder regions  
4. Cold dense air flows back toward warmer regions near the cloud layer  
  
This creates a large-scale planetary circulation loop.  
  
Because of rotation and atmospheric dynamics, this loop breaks into several **wind belts** rather than a single straight flow.  
  
---  
  
## Wind Belts  
  
Wind belts are long, stable atmospheric currents that move air horizontally across the planet.  
  
Characteristics:  
  
- relatively consistent direction  
- stable over long time periods  
- major drivers of island movement  
- define common travel routes  
  
Different belts may move:  
  
- northward  
- southward  
- east–west  
- or in looping circulation patterns  
  
These belts behave similarly to **ocean currents on Earth**.  
  
They create the world’s drifting geography, with islands moving along atmospheric lanes.  
  
---  
  
## Storm Belts  
  
Where air masses from different wind belts collide, **storm corridors** form.  
  
These regions are characterized by:  
  
- strong vertical convection  
- heavy rainfall  
- frequent lightning and turbulence  
- rapidly shifting weather systems  
  
Storm belts tend to form:  
  
- below the humid region south of the bright pole  
- at boundaries between major wind belts  
- along strong temperature gradients  
  
These areas are important sources of rainfall for the temperate regions.  
  
---  
  
## Calm Zones  
  
Between strong wind belts there are areas of **relatively stable air**.  
  
These calm zones often experience:  
  
- lighter winds  
- more predictable weather  
- fewer large storms  
  
Because of this stability, calm zones often become locations for:  
  
- major settlements  
- trade hubs  
- long-lived cultures  
  
They act as the atmospheric equivalent of **oceanic doldrums**.  
  
---  
  
## Interaction With Island Drift  
  
Floating islands move with the surrounding air currents.  
  
As a result:  
  
- islands in strong wind belts drift rapidly  
- islands in calmer regions move slowly  
- intersections of wind belts create complex drift paths  
  
This atmospheric structure explains why island movement follows **predictable lanes rather than random wandering**.  
  
---  
  
## Climate Influence  
  
Atmospheric circulation determines where moisture travels and where storms occur.  
  
As air moves away from the bright pole it carries moisture outward, eventually releasing it as rainfall when the air cools or when storm systems form.  
  
Because circulation varies between belts, different regions of the planet receive different amounts of precipitation.  
  
This creates the wide range of climates seen within the temperate belt.  
  
---  
  
## Summary  
  
Atmospheric circulation in this world is shaped by three main factors:  
  
- temperature differences between bright and dark poles  
- large-scale wind belts  
- storm corridors where air masses collide  
  
Together these systems move heat and moisture across the planet, shaping climate, weather, and the drifting movement of the floating islands.
