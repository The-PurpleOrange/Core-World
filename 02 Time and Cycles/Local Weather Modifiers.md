## Overview  
  
While the planet’s climate follows broad **north–south temperature bands**, the weather experienced on individual islands is strongly influenced by **local environmental conditions and atmospheric belt position**.  
  
These modifiers determine rainfall, wind strength, and seasonal variation.  
  
Because islands drift through atmospheric lanes and wind belts slowly shift over long periods, the local climate of a region may gradually change over decades or centuries.  
  
---  
  
## Altitude  
  
Altitude affects temperature, wind exposure, and humidity.  
  
General trends:  
  
Near the cloud layer  
  
- very humid air  
- frequent fog  
- condensation on surfaces  
- occasional drizzle  
  
These environments often receive moisture through **fog drip**, allowing vegetation to survive even with limited rainfall.  
  
Mid-altitude islands  
  
- balanced rainfall  
- moderate humidity  
- stable agricultural climates  
  
High-altitude islands  
  
- thinner air  
- stronger winds  
- lower humidity  
- cooler temperatures  
  
High islands are typically **drier and windier** than islands closer to the cloud layer.  
  
---  
  
## Windward and Leeward Effects  
  
When moist air encounters large islands or elevated terrain, it is forced upward.  
  
Windward side (facing incoming wind)  
  
- rising air cools  
- clouds form  
- rainfall increases  
  
Leeward side (downwind side)  
  
- descending air warms  
- clouds dissipate  
- rainfall decreases  
  
This creates **rain shadow regions**, where one side of an island may be lush while the opposite side is relatively dry.  
  
---  
  
## Storm Track Position  
  
Some regions lie within **regular storm corridors** created by atmospheric circulation.  
  
Islands within storm tracks often experience:  
  
- frequent rainfall  
- periodic strong winds  
- rapidly changing weather  
  
Islands outside these corridors may experience:  
  
- longer dry periods  
- calmer conditions  
- lower annual precipitation  
  
Storm tracks are one of the main sources of rainfall across the temperate belt.  
  
---  
  
## Island Size  
  
Island size influences how much weather it can generate locally.  
  
Large islands  
  
- can create their own convection  
- produce local rainfall systems  
- generate rain shadows  
- moderate temperature swings  
  
Small islands  
  
- depend more heavily on surrounding atmospheric conditions  
- cannot easily generate their own weather systems  
  
---  
  
## Proximity to Moisture Sources  
  
Moisture on the planet originates primarily from the **cloud sea**.  
  
Regions where vertical updrafts frequently lift moisture from the cloud layer may experience:  
  
- higher humidity  
- more cloud cover  
- increased rainfall  
  
Areas farther from these updraft zones tend to remain drier.  
  
---  
  
## Atmospheric Belt Position  
  
Wind belts and storm corridors slowly shift position over time.  
  
Short-term variation  
  
During each seasonal cycle (Turning), belts may shift slightly north or south, causing temporary changes in rainfall and weather patterns.  
  
Long-term variation  
  
Over decades or centuries, atmospheric belts may gradually reposition due to changes in circulation patterns.  
  
These slow shifts can cause:  
  
- formerly fertile regions becoming drier  
- grasslands expanding or shrinking  
- forests advancing or retreating  
  
Because of this, long-term climate patterns may change within recorded history.  
  
---  
  
## Summary  
  
Local climates are shaped by several interacting factors:  
  
- altitude relative to the cloud layer  
- windward and leeward rain shadow effects  
- position relative to storm tracks  
- island size and terrain  
- proximity to atmospheric moisture updrafts  
- long-term shifts in atmospheric belts  
  
Together these modifiers create diverse regional climates within the planet’s larger horizontal climate bands.
