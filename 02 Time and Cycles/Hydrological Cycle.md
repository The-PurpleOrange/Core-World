## Overview  
  
Water on this world is stored primarily in the **global cloud sea** beneath the floating islands.  
  
This layer functions as the planet’s main moisture reservoir, similar to an ocean on Earth.    
Water rises from the cloud sea through fog lift, storm updrafts, and atmospheric circulation from the hot poles main atmospheric injector, then eventually returns as rainfall or condensation.  
  
Because most land exists above the cloud layer, the hydrological cycle operates **vertically rather than horizontally**, with moisture constantly moving between the cloud sea and the sky.  
  
---  
  
## Primary Water Source  
  
The cloud sea supplies nearly all atmospheric moisture.  
  
Moisture enters the atmosphere through several mechanisms:  
  
- **fog lift** — slow upward movement of humid air near the cloud surface    
- **thermal updrafts** — rising warm air pulling moisture upward    
- **storm convection** — powerful vertical storms that carry water high into the atmosphere    
- **spray uplift** — turbulent storms occasionally throwing droplets above the cloud layer    
  
These processes continuously feed moisture into the sky above the clouds.  
  
---  
  
## Moisture Transport  
  
Once lifted into the atmosphere, water vapor is transported by the planet’s **wind belts and atmospheric circulation systems**.  
  
Moist air originating near the hot northern region spreads outward through the upper atmosphere before cooling and condensing elsewhere.  
  
Because multiple wind belts move in different directions, moisture can travel:  
  
- north to south    
- south to north    
- laterally along atmospheric lanes  
  
This produces a complex distribution of rainfall rather than a single global rain pattern.  
  
---  
  
## Rainfall Formation  
  
Rain occurs when moist air cools or is forced upward.  
  
Common rainfall triggers include:  
  
- storm belt convection  
- collisions between air masses  
- rising air over large islands  
- cooling air traveling toward colder regions  
  
As moisture condenses into droplets, it falls as:  
  
- rain  
- drizzle  
- mist  
- occasionally snow in colder regions  
  
Rainfall replenishes surface water on islands and supports ecosystems.  
  
---  
  
## Fog and Condensation  
  
Close to the cloud sea, islands often experience heavy fog.  
  
In these regions, moisture can collect directly on surfaces through **condensation and fog drip**.  
  
This allows vegetation to obtain water even when rainfall is infrequent.  
  
Fog-driven ecosystems are likely common near the lower altitudes of the habitable belt.  
  
---  
  
## Moisture Loss and Dry Regions  
  
Some areas receive little rainfall because moisture has already been removed from the air.  
  
Dry conditions tend to occur in:  
  
- regions far from storm belts  
- high altitude islands with thin air  
- areas dominated by descending dry air masses  
- rain shadow regions behind large islands  
  
These environments may support grasslands, scrublands, or sparse vegetation.  
  
---  
  
## Water Return to the Cloud Sea  
  
Eventually, most water returns to the cloud layer.  
  
This occurs through:  
  
- runoff from islands  
- falling precipitation  
- condensation droplets drifting downward  
- storm downdrafts carrying moisture back toward the cloud sea  
  
The cycle then begins again as moisture is lifted back into the atmosphere.  
  
---  
  
## Hydrological Balance  
  
The global water cycle maintains a continuous exchange between:  
  
- the **cloud sea**  
- the **atmosphere**  
- the **floating islands**  
  
Key principles:  
  
- the cloud sea acts as the main water reservoir  
- atmospheric circulation distributes moisture across the planet  
- rainfall and condensation sustain life on the islands  
  
This vertical hydrological cycle is one of the defining environmental systems of the world.
