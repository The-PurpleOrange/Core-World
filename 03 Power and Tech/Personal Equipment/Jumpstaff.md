**Concept**

- a **portable vaulting pole** you _keep with you_ (doesn’t get left behind)
- used like a mix of pole-vault + long-jump + walking staff

**Build (tribal → modern)**

- tribal: **laminated reeds + tough vine wrap + resin** (springy, light, repairable)
- later: **layered hardwood / horn strips / sky-iron ferrules**
- modern: **spring-steel spine with wood/cloth sheath** (still “looks” simple)

**How it’s used**

- run-up on the last few metres
- plant the tip in a **socket hole** or crack near the edge
- pole bends, stores energy, then whips you upward/forward
- you _pull the pole through_ (hands slide) and carry it on landing

**What makes it reliable**

- **edge sockets** become common infrastructure on travel routes (carved holes, iron rings)
- **tip options**: rock spike / hook / rubber pad depending on surface
- **safety line**: short wrist tether so you don’t lose it if you get yanked

**Range (no glider, trained user)**

- typical useful gap: **3–8 m**
- big show-off or elite courier: **10–15 m**
- it’s not a 50–200 m solution by itself (that needs glide or lines)

**Fails when**

- surface is crumbly / no good plant point
- Crosswinds / gusts mess your arc
- wet mossy edges cause the tip to slip (big hazard)

**Why early people could do it**

- it’s basically “staff + spear + climbing pole” tech
- easy to make from local materials
- skill-based, not resource-based
