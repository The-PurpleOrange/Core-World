## Concept

A Jumpstaff is a portable vaulting pole designed to help travellers cross gaps, climb ledges, and move through broken terrain.

It functions as a hybrid of:
- vaulting pole
- walking staff
- climbing pole

Unlike the mechanical [[Jump Harness]] system, the jumpstaff relies entirely on the flexibility of the pole and the user’s skill.

The pole is carried through the jump and used repeatedly during travel.

---

## Construction

Early jumpstaffs were built from flexible natural materials.

Common designs include:

**Tribal construction**
- laminated reeds or flexible wood
- tough vine or sinew wrapping
- resin sealing for durability

**Later improvements**
- layered hardwood cores
- horn or bone reinforcement strips
- metal ferrules on the tip and butt

---

## Use

A jumpstaff is typically used with a running approach.

Basic technique:
- the user runs the final meters toward a gap or ledge
- the staff tip is planted in a socket, crack, or firm ground
- the pole bends under load
- stored elastic energy launches the user upward and forward
- the user pulls the staff free and carries it through the landing

---

## Infrastructure

On well-traveled routes, jumpstaff users benefit from prepared planting points.

Common features include:
- carved socket holes near cliff edges
- iron rings embedded in stone
- reinforced planting stones
- worn grooves from repeated staff use

---

## Tip Variations

Jumpstaff tips are adapted for different terrain.

Common designs include:
- rock spike for cracks and stone
- hooked tip for ledges
- flat pad for packed soil
- rubber or leather pad for smooth surfaces

---

## Safety Measures

To avoid losing the staff during a jump, some users attach:
- short wrist tethers
- sliding grip wraps
- reinforced handle sections

---

## Performance

With a trained user and good terrain:

Typical useful gap
- 3–8 m

Experienced courier or expert
- 10–15 m

Distances depend heavily on:
- run-up speed
- planting surface
- pole flexibility
- user techniques

---

## Limitations

Jumpstaffs become unreliable when:
- the surface is crumbly
- there is no solid planting point
- strong crosswinds disrupt the jump
- moss or wet stone causes tip slippage

---
## Spring-Assisted Jumpstaff Addendum

Some jumpstaff designs incorporate a lockable internal compression spring in the lower shaft.  
When the staff is planted and compressed during the jump, the spring stores energy and releases it at takeoff, adding extra impulse to the user's natural vault.

This system supplements both:
- the user’s leg drive
- the natural flex of the staff

The added boost increases gap distance while keeping the device small enough to remain a handheld tool.

#### Phosphor Bronze Spring
Energy added per jump
- ~0.3–0.5 kJ
Typical jump range (trained user, run-up)
- normal jumpstaff: 3–8 m
- spring-assisted: ~4–9 m

#### Spring Steel Spring
Energy added per jump
- ~0.6–1.0 kJ
Typical jump range (trained user, run-up)
- normal jumpstaff: 3–8m
- spring-assisted: ~5–11m
