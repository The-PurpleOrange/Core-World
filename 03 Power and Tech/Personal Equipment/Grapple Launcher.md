### Overview

The grapple launcher is a compact personal anchoring device used during the final phase of island travel.  
It fires a lightweight dart or hook attached to a thin line, allowing the traveler to guide their approach, stabilize their glide path, and reduce forward speed before landing.

The grapple launcher is **not intended to lift or pull the user upward**. Instead, it acts as a **guidance and braking tool** used in combination with gliders, jump harnesses, and landing boots.

Typical uses include:

- stabilizing glide approach
- guiding toward landing structures
- reducing forward velocity
- committing to a landing point
- emergency ground-drag braking

---

## System Layout

A typical grapple system consists of:

- wrist or forearm mounted launcher
- projectile dart or folding hook
- braided pilot line
- forearm reel and braking system
- shoulder redirect guide
- torso harness anchor

To prevent the user from being spun or injured by line tension, the grapple line passes through a **shoulder redirect guide** mounted near the top of the harness.

dart → launcher → shoulder redirect → torso harness anchor

This arrangement ensures that tension aligns with the user’s center of mass rather than pulling sideways on the arm.

---

## Projectile Types

Several projectile types exist depending on terrain and route infrastructure.

### Toggle Dart

A narrow dart that passes through a ring or socket and deploys a small crossbar under tension.

Best for:
- prepared route rings
- travel infrastructure
- reliable anchor points

---

### Folding Hook

A compact hook that folds during flight and opens when tension is applied.

Best for:
- rails
- beams
- branches
- natural terrain features

---

### Drag Hook

A specialized ground anchor used to create drag during landing.

Instead of anchoring solidly, it bites into soil or vegetation and drags across the surface, producing controlled resistance.

Best for:
- emergency braking
- wilderness travel
- uncontrolled landing approaches

---
### Anchor Dart

A piercing ground anchor designed to penetrate forward and resist removal.

While slim in flight, after being buried and having tension applied several flukes pivot outward to resist extraction. Though too much force can quickly dislodge the dart.
These darts often come in disposable cartridges 

best for:
- forward direction breaking by allowing to the user to create more drag with a [[Glider]] by arching away from the dart
- when the goal is to continue moving
- uncontrolled landing approaches

---

## Range

Typical grapple launcher ranges:

| Shot Type         | Distance |
| ----------------- | -------- |
| reliable shot     | 10–15 m  |
| skilled shot      | 15–20 m  |

The grapple is normally fired **late in the glide approach**, when the landing point is already visible and close.

---

## Braking System

The line spool contains a friction braking system that resists rapid payout.

This converts kinetic energy into heat and gradually reduces the user’s forward speed.

Typical braking forces:

|braking level|force|
|---|---|
|light guidance|200–300 N|
|normal braking|300–500 N|
|strong braking|500–800 N|

This is sufficient to guide and slow a glide approach but not intended to arrest a full fall.

The braking system may include:

- friction drum
- adjustable drag control
- mechanical governor
- emergency release mechanism

---

## Route Infrastructure

To support frequent travel, many island routes include prepared grapple structures:

- landing rings
- guide rails
- anchor sockets
- landing towers
- netted platforms

These features allow travellers to reliably secure their grapples during approach.

---

## Limitations

The grapple launcher is not designed to:
- pull the user upward
- lift full body weight vertically
- anchor reliably in loose soil
- replace climbing equipment

It functions primarily as a **glide guidance and braking device**.

Proper landing still depends on:
- good approach control
- correct firing timing

---

## Travel Sequence

A typical island crossing using modern travel equipment follows this sequence:

1. run-up and launch using [[Jump Harness]] or [[Jumpstaff]]
2. deploy backpack [[Glider]]
3. glide toward target island
4. fire grapple launcher during final approach
5. grapple line engages anchor point
6. braking reel stabilizes and slows approach
7. [[Landing Boots]] absorb touchdown
8. grapple is retrieved and rewound
