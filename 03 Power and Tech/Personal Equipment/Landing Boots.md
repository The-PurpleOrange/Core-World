## Overview

Landing boots are mechanical shock-absorbing footwear designed to safely absorb impact energy when landing from jumps, glides, or short falls during island travel.

They allow travellers to land from heights that would normally cause injury by **increasing the stopping distance of the body** and **controlling the deceleration force**.

Landing boots became common equipment once island-hopping travel became widespread.

Typical users include:
- couriers
- miners
- island scouts
- patrol units
- travellers moving between small island clusters    

---

# Core Principle

The boots reduce injury by **increasing stopping distance during landing**.

Impact force is reduced when deceleration occurs over a longer distance.

The governing relationship is:

Work = Force × Distance

and

mgh = Work

Where:

- m = mass of jumper
- g = gravity
- h = fall height

Increasing the **compression distance** spreads the force over time, lowering peak impact.

---

# Mechanical Layout

Each boot typically contains:

- telescoping compression strut
- internal spring stack
- mechanical damping system
- deployment latch
- locking hinge system
- reinforced heel plate
- energy absorption pad

The compression system sits primarily in the **heel and calf region** of the boot.

---

# Deployment

Boots normally remain **collapsed for walking**.

When the user enters a fall state, a trigger mechanism deploys the suspension struts.

Deployment methods may include:

- inertial trigger
- manual toggle
- fall-distance latch
- tether release from other equipment

Once deployed, the landing struts extend to their full travel length.
Landing locks the boots back into a collapsed state

---

# Compression Stroke

Typical travel distance:

15 cm boot suspension

During landing, the user’s legs also bend naturally.

Typical total stopping distance:

boot compression: ~15 cm  
leg compression: ~30–40 cm  
total stopping distance: ~45–55 cm

---

# Landing Behaviour

Proper landing technique:
1. feet contact ground
2. knees bend
3. boot struts compress
4. body lowers smoothly into a crouch
5. springs absorb remaining energy until boots full compress and re-lock

The user typically ends in a **deep crouch**, not seated or falling backward.

Incorrect posture or uneven ground can still cause injury.

# Internal Energy Systems (Optional Designs)

Several mechanical energy absorption systems exist.

### Spring Compression

Most common system.

Energy stored as: E = ½ k x²

Pros:
- simple
- reliable

Cons:
- returns some bounce energy

---

### Flywheel Absorption (advanced variant)

Landing motion spins a flywheel via a gear train.

Pros:
- smoother deceleration
- less rebound

Cons:
- heavier
- more complex

---

### Friction Dampers

Energy converted directly to heat through braking surfaces.

Pros:
- stable landing
- simple

Cons:
- wear components

---

# Advantages

Landing boots provide:
- safer landings
- reduced joint stress
- survivable glider descents
- improved mobility between islands

They are lightweight enough for daily wear.

---

# Limitations

Landing boots do not make the user immune to falling hazards.

Limitations include:
- uneven terrain
- sideways landings
- bottoming out at extreme heights
- mechanical failure

They reduce injury risk but **do not eliminate it**.

---

# Cultural Impact

Because island travel often requires vertical movement, landing boots became widely adopted.

Boot maintenance and spring replacement are routine parts of travel preparation.
