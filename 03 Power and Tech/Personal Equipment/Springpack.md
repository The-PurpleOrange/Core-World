- Springpacks
	Wearable jump-assist device using **pre-wound mechanical springs**
    Worn like a **backpack + climbing harness**
    Designed to provide a **controlled launch boost**

---

- Mechanical Layout
    
    - Pack frame mounted to **hip belt + leg loops**
    
    - **Spring bank** stored vertically in the pack
    
    - **Deployable push-strut**
        - telescoping pole under the pack
        - ~30–40 cm stroke
        - rubber or metal foot pad
            
    - **Trigger + governor**
        - prevents instant “kick”
        - releases energy progressively
            

---

- Force Path
	springs → push strut → ground → pack frame → harness → hips → body

---

- Operating Sequence
    
    1. user takes a running start
    2. trigger activates
    3. push-strut plants on the ground
    4. springs release through the strut
    5. pack pushes upward through the harness
    6. strut retracts and system must be rewound

---

- Key Engineering Constraints
    - Requires **long stroke** to prevent excessive forces
    - Typical stroke: **30–40 cm**
    - Uses:
        - cams
        - governors
        - torque limiters

---

- Basic Physics
    Energy → launch velocity
    v = √(2E / m)
    
    Where
    - `v` = launch velocity
    - `E` = spring energy
    - `m` = total mass of jumper

---

- Range Contribution
    
    Assuming all spring energy converts to vertical motion
    h = E / (m g)
    
    Where
    - `R` = horizontal range
    - `v` = launch velocity
    - `g` = 9.8 m/s²

---

- Height Contribution
    
    Assuming ideal 0° launch angle**
    R = v² / g
    
	Where
	- `E` = spring energy
	- `m` = mass (75 kg)
	- `g` = 9.8 m/s²

---

- Performance Assumptions
    
    jumper mass = 75 kg  
    launch angle = 45°  
    air drag ignored
    

---

- 1 kJ Springpack (courier grade)
    
    Velocity:
    
    v = √(2 × 1000 / 75)  
    v ≈ 5.2 m/s
    
    Range contribution:
    
    R = 5.2² / 9.8  
    R ≈ 2.7 m
    
    Vertical boost (directly up):
	H ≈ 0.5 + 1.3–1.4 m    
	
    Result:
    
    **≈ 2–3 m additional horizontal distance**
    

---

- 2 kJ Springpack (industrial)
    
    Velocity:
    
    v = √(2 × 2000 / 75)  
    v ≈ 7.3 m/s
    
    Range contribution:
    
    R = 7.3² / 9.8  
    R ≈ 5.4 m
    
    Vertical boost (directly up):
	H ≈ 0.5 + 2.7 m    
    
    Result:
    
    **≈ 5–6 m additional horizontal distance**
    

---

- Practical Gap Distances
    
    If a trained island-hopper can normally clear **4–6 m**
    
    |System|Practical gap|
    |---|---|
    |No gear|4–6 m|
    |+ 1 kJ springpack|7–9 m|
    |+ 2 kJ springpack|9–12 m|
    

---

- Advantages vs Jumpstaff
    
    - Works from **very small platforms**
    - No need for **plant holes or sockets**
    - **Consistent output**
    - Hands remain **free for balance or grapples**
    - Useful for **upward hops and ledge climbs**

---

- Drawbacks
    
    - **Heavier and bulkier**
    - Requires **rewinding**
    - More **mechanical complexity**
    - Still cannot cross **large gaps** without gliding or cables

---

- Typical Users
    
    - couriers
    - scouts
    - miners
    - patrol units
    - workers operating on **small unstable rock platforms**
