## Core Principle

Large-scale atmospheric circulation organizes into several stable wind belts across the floating island layer.

These belts form from three interacting forces:

- constant heating from the permanently illuminated polar region
- large-scale convection rising from the cloud sea
- planetary rotation

The bright polar region receives the strongest corelight, heating the lower atmosphere and driving powerful rising air currents. At the same time, heat and moisture rising from the cloud sea create persistent convection columns.

As this air spreads outward and later cools, enormous atmospheric circulation cells form. These circulation systems produce stable wind belts that move air horizontally across the island layer.

These belts determine:

- island drift directions
- storm formation zones
- travel routes
- regional climates

Because floating islands are exposed to these winds, the atmosphere functions much like an ocean current system.

---

# Five Belt Model

The atmosphere organizes into five major wind belts across the habitable sky.

Each belt forms a large circulation zone where wind direction, speed, and weather patterns remain relatively stable.

These belts act as the primary drivers of island drift.

From coreward toward the outer sky:

1. Inner Trade Belt
2. Inner Calm Belt
3. Mid-Latitude Westerlies
4. Outer Storm Belt
5. Polar Drift Belt

Each belt produces distinct environmental conditions and patterns of island movement.

---

# Belt 1 — Inner Trade Belt

## Location

Between the bright polar convection zone and the inner calm belt.

## Wind Direction

Strong, consistent flow moving away from the bright pole.

## Characteristics

- steady directional winds
- relatively stable weather
- moderate storm activity
- strong and predictable drift currents

## Consequences

- major trade routes form here
- belt islands follow long predictable migration paths
- glider travel is easier due to reliable airflow
- large drifting island belts often occupy this region

These winds act as one of the world's primary atmospheric highways.

---

# Belt 2 — Inner Calm Belt

## Location

Between the inner trade belt and the mid-latitude westerlies.

## Wind Behavior

Weak and variable horizontal winds.

## Characteristics

- slow drifting air
- frequent fog banks
- long calm periods
- occasional sudden storm development
- persistent cloud formations

## Mechanism

Air flowing from neighboring belts slows and begins to rise vertically in this region. Horizontal wind energy decreases, producing extended calm conditions.

## Consequences

- anchored islands commonly develop here
- dense ecosystems thrive due to stable moisture
- sailing and gliding travel become difficult
- large long-lived settlements often develop

During Fog Season these calm regions can become vast drifting fog basins.

---

# Belt 3 — Mid-Latitude Westerlies

## Location

Between the calm belt and the outer storm belt.

## Wind Direction

Strong flow moving in the opposite direction of the inner trades.

## Characteristics

- strong steady winds
- relatively stable airflow corridors
- moderate storm activity
- powerful high altitude currents

## Consequences

- major migration corridor for drifting islands
- long-distance trade routes often follow these currents
- excellent gliding conditions due to strong winds

Many large drifting island belts travel within this zone.

---

# Belt 4 — Outer Storm Belt

## Location

Region where descending cooler air collides with rising warm air from lower atmospheric circulation.

## Characteristics

- powerful storm systems
- violent wind shifts
- large turbulence zones
- strong updraft towers rising from the cloud sea

## Storm Behavior

Storm systems here form where atmospheric circulation cells interact and vertical convection from the cloud sea intensifies.

Storm activity increases dramatically during:

- Storm Season (Bright Surge)
- the Rebright phase later in the Turning

## Consequences

- dangerous navigation
- lightning storms and heavy rainfall
- powerful updraft columns
- seasonal storm energy harvesting

This belt is one of the most dangerous atmospheric regions in the world.

---

# Belt 5 — Polar Drift Belt

## Location

Outer atmospheric regions far from the bright polar heating zone.

## Characteristics

- cold descending air
- strong outward winds
- thin atmospheric moisture
- reduced cloud formation

## Consequences

- sparse ecosystems
- isolated drifting islands
- extreme wind exposure
- difficult travel conditions

Few large settlements exist here due to the harsh climate and distance from stable trade routes.

# Vertical Structure

Wind strength varies significantly by altitude.

## High Altitude

- strongest winds
- fastest island drift lanes
- dangerous turbulence during storms

## Island Layer

- moderate winds
- most settlements exist here
- stable long-term drift patterns

## Cloud Edge

- slower winds
- heavy fog interaction
- frequent rising convection from the cloud sea

Wind direction generally remains consistent across these altitude layers within a belt. However, higher layers move faster than lower ones.

Because islands float at slightly different heights depending on lift-mineral concentration and mass, nearby islands may experience different wind speeds even within the same belt.

This produces slow separation between islands rather than chaotic divergence.

---

# Interaction With Floating Islands

Wind belts strongly influence island movement.

Island types interact differently with atmospheric currents.

## Anchored Islands

- barely affected by winds
- often remain within calm belts

## Cluster Islands

- drift slowly with regional airflow
- may remain grouped within slow wind zones

## Belt Islands

- captured by strong atmospheric drift lanes
- migrate thousands of kilometers over time

## Drifter Islands

- small fragments caught between circulation zones
- follow unpredictable paths

These interactions create a constantly shifting geography where island positions change over time.

# Storm Corridors

Storm corridors form where atmospheric circulation systems interact.

Common locations include:

- belt boundaries
- strong convection zones rising from the cloud sea
- regions where wind layers shear against each other

Storm corridors can produce:

- lightning towers rising from the cloud sea
- heavy rainfall events
- violent turbulence
- powerful vertical updraft columns

Although dangerous, some civilizations harvest energy from these storms.

---

# Cultural Impact

Because wind belts control island drift and travel conditions:

- maps track wind lanes rather than fixed land positions
- trade routes follow predictable atmospheric corridors
- cities form where wind systems intersect
- navigation requires understanding seasonal wind shifts

Knowledge of atmospheric currents becomes essential for sailors, navigators, explorers, and skyfarers.

Wind prediction is one of the most valuable scientific disciplines in the world.
