## Cloud Sea Depth

Average cloud sea depth: ~6 km

This layer forms a massive atmospheric ocean separating the floating island layer from the hotter lower atmosphere near the core.

The cloud sea is deep enough that its interior behaves like a separate environment with its own storms, currents, and turbulence.

---

## Vertical Structure

Upper Cloud Zone (0–1 km below islands)

Properties
- heavy fog banks
- cloud tops and towers
- frequent condensation
- occasional rainfall onto islands
- strong vertical convection

Most islands stabilize just above or partially within this layer.

---

Mid Cloud Ocean (1–5 km depth)

Properties
- extremely dense cloud mass
- violent turbulence
- rotating storm cells
- near zero visibility
- strong vertical updrafts and downdrafts

Navigation here is extremely difficult and dangerous.

This region behaves like a **true atmospheric ocean interior**.

---

Lower Cloud Boundary (5–6 km depth)

Properties
- rapidly warming air
- intense updraft columns
- rising vapor plumes from below
- unstable air pressure

These updrafts constantly feed moisture upward into the cloud sea.

---

## Sub-Cloud Region

Below the cloud sea lies a hotter and more hostile atmospheric zone closer to the planetary core.

Characteristics

- strong rising heat currents
- turbulent airflow
- drifting debris fields
- dense concentrations of metal-rich meteors

Because these bodies contain little lift mineral, they cannot remain suspended above the cloud sea and accumulate below it.

---

## Mining Operations

Some nations conduct extremely dangerous **cloud dive mining operations**.

Typical method:

1. ships or staffed lightning cages descend through storm corridors
2. harpoon lines are fired into passing metal bodies
3. ore fragments are hauled upward before turbulence separates the vessel

These dives are rare, hazardous, and usually conducted by heavily equipped industrial or military ships.

Successful dives can recover:

- meteoric iron
- nickel-rich metals
- rare dense alloys
